What We Do

With the help of other members of the Wheaton community and individuals living with Type 1 Diabetes (T1D), we observed a few key problems with existing personalized diabetes management:

  1. Patients are not receiving alerts of low and high blood glucose readings smartly -- in short, there is a lack of prediction-based alerts that reflect their personal historical trends.
  2. While patients may be able to add qualitative information pertaining to certain points in time depending on the devices they wear, there is currently no systematic or meaningful way to facilitate and solicit information about critical points in time that could leverage the timing of future alerts.
  3. Lastly, the statistical reports we’ve seen generated from continuous glucose monitoring data are long and confusing. These reports lack a necessary level of interaction that enables a patient to explore their data in ways that reveal patterns that provide insights for alternate management strategies.

So, we set out to address these problems by developing prototype systems and software to facilitate the review and management of the “data exhaust” that accompanies those living with T1D, from databases to statistical and machine learning algorithms to interactive visualizations of past histories via web and mobile apps.

Login

1. The GluGo logo is front and center upon opening the app. Below that, are the fields for email and password.

2. The 'Sign in' button allows the user to login to their account and view their personalized data. Any changes made to their data will be saved and associaed with their account.

Graphing

1. This bar selects the time span shown on the graph, and displays the glucose values that fall in the selected period.

2. Each point on the graph represents a unique glucose value from the user's dataset. Points are color-coded to signify what type of event occurred near the time of the reading. A legend containg the available event types is in the upper-right hand side of the graph.

3. The 'prev' and 'next' buttons change the displayed time span, according to the selection in the bar above the graph. For example, if 'day' is selected and the user presses the 'next' button, the next day will be displayed on the graph.

4. The 'Add Activity' field allows the user to edit event types and add comments to further personalize their data. This selction transitions to the next screen.

Glucose Readings

1. The 'Back' button redirects to the previous page containing the graph with, a smooth transitioning animation.

2. Each glucose value from the user's dataset is given a unique selection that displays the glucose reading, in mg/dL, and the date followed by the time the reading took place on. Entries are color-coded in the same fasion as the points on the graph to signify even type.

3. The plus icon allows for custom input of a new glucose value, which will be added to the list.

4. Selecting a field will bring the user to our final page, 'Activity Management', described in the following section.

Why We Do It

Type 1 Diabetes (T1D) is a severe and wide-reaching disease.
  1. According to the JDRF, almost a quarter of a million youth under age 20 and over a million adults live with T1D, with numbers projected to increase five-fold by 2050;
  2. The World Health Organization estimates 1.5 million deaths are caused by diabetes, making it the 8th leading cause of death;
  3. Despite advances in insulin delivery and blood glucose sensing hardware, fewer than one-third of people with T1D in the U.S. achieve target blood glucose control levels;
  4. Grassroots solutions are needed to facilitate the convergence of hybrid close-loop systems and advances in machine learning that can leverage the glut of data toward more personalized management strategies.
Several of our team members have loved ones living with diabetes. As we learned more about the struggles of living with finger pricks, glucose sensors, insulin pumps, injections, and mountains of data, we knew this was an area in which we could improve their lives.

Who We Are

We are undergraduate students at Wheaton College (Norton, Massachusetts) enrolled in an experimental, cross-disciplinary class working to develop an integrated set of applications to ease the process of managing diabetes. In collaboration with three individuals with T1D who are sharing their data, we seek to make their data more personalized and relevant so that patients and doctors can tailor treatment to their specific needs.

We are a diverse group. We major in Computer Science, Mathematics, Bioinformatics, Political Science, Art, and Business. We come from all over the world, each with very different plans for our lives after college. However, we all share a passion for elegant and efficient design and development; leveraging new technologies, software, and ideas to help people.

Many of us will be graduating this year and plan to take the skills, ideas, and products we’ve developed over this semester to the next level in each of our respective fields. This experimental course has been a spark for next generation methodologies and products whose lifetimes will endure long beyond the end of one semester.
